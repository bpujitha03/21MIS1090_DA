Big Impact of Sanitation and clean water

Save Water at Home

Turn off the tap while brushing your teeth or washing dishes to save up to 10 liters of water per minute. Fix leaks immediately to conserve resources.

Reuse Water

Reuse greywater from washing machines or basins to water plants or clean outdoor spaces. Every drop reused is a step towards sustainability. Water reuse, also known as water recycling or reclamation, is the process of treating and reusing water from various sources

Protect Water Sources

Avoid disposing of chemicals and plastics in water bodies. Join community clean-ups to preserve the purity of local rivers and lakes. Conserve water: Use less water by taking shorter showers, fixing leaks, and installing water-saving devices. Reduce pollution: Use fewer fertilizers and pesticides, and dispose of hazardous products properly.

Adopt Water-Saving Technology

Install low-flow showerheads and dual-flush toilets. Use water-efficient appliances to reduce your water footprint. Implementing rainwater harvesting and greywater recycling systems. And, start embracing sustainable landscaping practices, we can significantly impact water conservation. Government policies and financial incentives further encourage businesses and individuals to adopt water-saving technologies and practices.

India's Water and Sanitation Initiatives

Swachh Bharat Mission

Launched in 2014, the Swachh Bharat Mission aimed to make India open defecation-free. Over 110 million toilets were constructed, improving health and hygiene across the nation.

Launched: October 2, 2014 Objective: To achieve universal sanitation coverage and eliminate open defecation.

Achievements:

Declared India open defecation-free (ODF) in 2019. Constructed over 110 million toilets in rural and urban areas. Promoted behavioral change with awareness campaigns to instill the importance of hygiene. Implemented initiatives for sustainable solid and liquid waste management.

Impact:

Improved health outcomes with a decline in waterborne diseases. Enhanced dignity and safety for women, especially in rural areas. Positive environmental effects, including reduced contamination of water sources.

Jal Jeevan Mission

Started in 2019, Jal Jeevan Mission focuses on providing functional household tap connections to every rural household by 2024, ensuring access to safe drinking water.


Launched: August 15, 2019 Objective: To provide functional household tap connections (FHTCs) to every rural household by 2024.

Features: Focus on water quality monitoring and testing to ensure safe drinking water. Promotes community participation in managing water resources. Integration with rainwater harvesting and groundwater recharge systems.

Achievements (as of 2025):

Over 12 crore households now have tap water connections. Reduced the burden on women and girls who earlier had to fetch water from long distances.

Namami Gange Program

The Namami Gange Program, launched in 2014, works to rejuvenate the Ganga River by improving wastewater management and reducing pollution levels.

Launched: 2014
Objective: To rejuvenate the Ganga River, India’s lifeline, by addressing pollution and improving sanitation in riverine communities.
Key Components: Infrastructure development for sewage treatment plants (STPs).
Promotion of sustainable agriculture and afforestation in the Ganga basin.
Public engagement through the “Ganga Task Force.”
Results: Reduction in Ganga’s pollution levels, with stretches of the river now meeting bathing standards. Treated over 4,800 MLD (million liters per day) of sewage through new and upgraded STPs.

Atal Bhujal Yojana (ABY) – Groundwater Management

Launched: December 25, 2019
Budget: ₹6,000 Crore (co-funded by the World Bank and the Government of India)
Coverage: 7 states - Gujarat, Haryana, Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Rajasthan, and Uttar Pradesh.
Objective: Address groundwater depletion and ensure sustainable groundwater management through community participation.

Key Strategies:
Village-Level Water Budgeting: Communities assess water availability and demand.
Efficient Irrigation: Promotes drip and sprinkler systems to reduce water wastage.
Water Conservation: Desilting tanks, pond restoration, and check dam construction.
Monitoring Tools: Use of GIS, remote sensing, and data sharing.
Community Training: Involves locals, especially women, in water conservation.

Achievements: Groundwater recharge projects have improved water availability. Farmers using micro-irrigation report less water usage and higher productivity. Awareness campaigns foster community responsibility for water resources.
